The idea, in plain language

The mine already measures everything. It just isn't understood fast enough.

Two kilometres underground, three things quietly cause harm: bad air, failing equipment, and mismatched ventilation. None announce themselves clearly. Today a human is expected to watch dozens of live numbers across many zones and connect the dots, every time, all shift. That's exactly the job a machine should help with.

☁

Bad air

Diesel exhaust (CO, NO₂), blasting gases, dust, and falling oxygen. Often you can't smell the danger until it's serious.

⚙

Failing equipment

Pumps, fans, and drills vibrate differently for days before they fail. Miss it, and a $500K breakdown, or worse, follows.

🌀

Wasted ventilation

Fans can be 40%+ of a mine's energy bill. Too much wastes money; too little is a safety risk. Matching airflow to people is hard by hand.

The gap we close

The sensors exist. The brain and the friendly face don't.

Maestro Digital Mine has air-quality stations running in Vale's Sudbury mines (including Creighton, 2.4 km deep) since 2015. Sandvik's Newtrax platform monitors Glencore's people and equipment underground. Vale runs a remote operations centre in Sudbury watching all five Ontario mines, with ventilation-on-demand saving millions.

So the data is there. What's missing is the AI interpretation layer that turns raw numbers into a prioritised, plain-English decision, and a practical operator interface built for the person on shift, not just an engineer. OreAcle is that missing layer, built with free open-source AI.

  • AI alert feed: what triggered, when, and the recommended response

What we use, and how little

Hardware: humble, plug-and-play, in the box

Everything comes from one SunFounder Raspberry Pi Pico W Ultimate Starter Kit. No soldering. Hardware is ~10% of the effort; the idea, the AI, and the interface are the stars.

RP2040

Raspberry Pi Pico W

The edge "nervous system"

  • CPUDual-core Arm Cortex-M0+ @ 133 MHz
  • Memory264 KB SRAM · 2 MB flash
  • Wireless2.4 GHz Wi-Fi (CYW43439)
  • LanguageMicroPython
  • Link to MacUSB serial (primary)

Myth-buster: Amazon lists "Tegra / FreeRTOS", that's an auto-fill error. It's an RP2040 microcontroller. It's too small to run the AI, which is exactly why the brain lives on the MacBook.

About This Demo

The physical potentiometer is a calibrated stand-in signal for a real airflow sensor. It is not measuring actual airflow — it demonstrates the full data pipeline: ADC reading → USB JSON → Web Serial → live dashboard update.

Signal processing: 50-sample ADC average → exponential smoothing (α = 0.08) → deadband filter (±0.10 m/s) → clamped 0–5 m/s range. Status thresholds: ≥ 1.7 m/s → OK · ≥ 0.8 m/s → WATCH · below → ALERT. Fan % rises as airflow falls.

Firmware: hardware/firmware/main_potentiometer.py — upload to Pico as main.py via Thonny.

Requires Chrome or Edge over http://localhost. Close Thonny first (port conflict), then click Connect to Pico and pick the usbmodem port.

The Problem

Two kilometres down, three hazards stay quiet

Bad air
Diesel exhaust produces CO and NO2. Blasting leaves toxic gases and dust. Oxygen drops without warning. A worker often cannot smell or feel the danger until it is already serious.
Failing equipment
A pump, fan, or drill vibrates differently days before it fails. If nobody catches the pattern it fails catastrophically. The signal is already in the data. Nobody is reading it.
Wasted ventilation
Ventilation is 40% or more of a mine's energy bill. Fans full blast everywhere waste millions. Too little air where people work is a safety crisis. Smarter control saves both.

The mine measures everything. It just is not understood fast enough, explained clearly enough, or acted on automatically enough.

The Gap

Data is not decisions

Gap 01
The AI interpretation layer is thin
Systems measure and display well. The leap from "here are the numbers" to "here is what is wrong, why, and what to do right now" still happens in a human's head, or is sold as an expensive add-on upgrade.
Gap 02
The interface is built for engineers
Dense dashboards assume you know what "38 ppm CO at 0.4 airflow" means. The frontline worker needs something clear: green, amber, red, what is happening, what to do, one action.

Vale and Glencore have the eyes (sensors) and the muscles (automation). OreAcle is the open-source, zero-cost brain and friendly face that connects them.

The Solution

Sense. Think. Explain. Act.

01 · Sense
Read the zone
A Raspberry Pi Pico W reads vibration, temperature, humidity and occupancy and streams them over USB serial. Gas values are injected in software; a real $10 gas sensor is a one-wire swap.
02 · Think
Spot the danger
An Isolation Forest (scikit-learn) watches every channel together and flags abnormal combinations. A safety rules engine checks hard thresholds such as CO limits and heat-stress levels.
03 · Explain
Say it in plain English
A local LLM (Ollama, Llama 3.2 3B, offline, $0) turns each anomaly into a short alert with the action and the reason, and powers a chat copilot that answers operator questions.
04 · Act
Close the loop
Ventilation-on-Demand picks the fan speed from occupancy plus air quality and sends the command back to the board. A real, visible closed loop: sense to think to act.
