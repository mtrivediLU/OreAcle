The idea, in plain language

The mine already measures everything. It just isn't understood fast enough.

Two kilometres underground, three things quietly cause harm: bad air, failing equipment, and mismatched ventilation. None announce themselves clearly. Today a human is expected to watch dozens of live numbers across many zones and connect the dots, every time, all shift. That's exactly the job a machine should help with.

☁

Bad air

Diesel exhaust (CO, NO₂), blasting gases, dust, and falling oxygen. Often you can't smell the danger until it's serious.

⚙

Failing equipment

Pumps, fans, and drills vibrate differently for days before they fail. Miss it, and a $500K breakdown, or worse, follows.

🌀

Wasted ventilation

Fans can be 40%+ of a mine's energy bill. Too much wastes money; too little is a safety risk. Matching airflow to people is hard by hand.

The gap we close

The sensors exist. The brain and the friendly face don't.

Maestro Digital Mine has air-quality stations running in Vale's Sudbury mines (including Creighton, 2.4 km deep) since 2015. Sandvik's Newtrax platform monitors Glencore's people and equipment underground. Vale runs a remote operations centre in Sudbury watching all five Ontario mines, with ventilation-on-demand saving millions.

So the data is there. What's missing is the AI interpretation layer that turns raw numbers into a prioritised, plain-English decision, and a practical operator interface built for the person on shift, not just an engineer. OreAcle is that missing layer, built with free open-source AI.

"Vale and Glencore have the eyes and the muscles. OreAcle is the open-source, zero-cost brain and friendly face that connects them, proved end-to-end on a $40 board."

What we use, and how little

Hardware: humble, plug-and-play, in the box

Everything comes from one SunFounder Raspberry Pi Pico W Ultimate Starter Kit. No soldering. Hardware is ~10% of the effort; the idea, the AI, and the interface are the stars.

RP2040

Raspberry Pi Pico W

The edge "nervous system"

  • CPUDual-core Arm Cortex-M0+ @ 133 MHz
  • Memory264 KB SRAM · 2 MB flash
  • Wireless2.4 GHz Wi-Fi (CYW43439)
  • LanguageMicroPython
  • Link to MacUSB serial (primary)

Myth-buster: Amazon lists "Tegra / FreeRTOS", that's an auto-fill error. It's an RP2040 microcontroller. It's too small to run the AI, which is exactly why the brain lives on the MacBook.

The AI: open-source, local, free

Two kinds of AI, zero dollars, no internet required

Everything runs on the laptop with no API keys and nothing leaving the machine, which matters underground, where connectivity is poor and data is sensitive.

Detection

Isolation Forest

scikit-learn

A lightweight, explainable machine-learning model that learns what "normal" looks like and flags abnormal combinations of readings, even when no single number crossed a hard limit.

  • Instant on CPU
  • Explainable
  • Backed by hard safety rules
Explanation + Copilot

Llama 3.2 (3B)

via Ollama · runs locally

A small open-source large language model turns each anomaly into a one-sentence alert with a recommended action, and powers a chat copilot you can ask in plain English.

  • $0 / offline
  • Tuned for an Intel-i9 / 16 GB Mac
  • gemma2:2b fast fallback
Optional

faster-whisper

speech-to-text

A stretch goal that lets a supervisor speak a question hands-free ("is Zone 3 safe?"). Runs on CPU, fully local.

  • Hands-free
  • Local
  • Add only after core is done

01
The Challenge

Underground mines are already data-rich. Interpretation is the missing layer.

Modern mines deploy CO sensors, vibration monitors, air-quality stations, and occupancy trackers continuously across every zone. A shift supervisor is expected to watch dozens of live numbers, pattern-match in real time, and decide: evacuate? increase ventilation? call maintenance? That cognitive overhead is exactly the job AI should handle.

Interpretation gap

Raw sensor numbers don't come with priority, context, or a recommended action. A reading is only meaningful once compared to thresholds, trend history, and zone context simultaneously.

Connectivity constraints

Underground mines have poor or no internet connectivity. Cloud AI tools are often unavailable at the point of decision and raise data-sovereignty concerns for sensitive operational data.

Cost unpredictability

Commercial AI APIs charge per token. A continuous 24/7 multi-zone deployment generating thousands of alerts per shift accumulates unpredictable, recurring subscription costs.

03
The Opportunity

Open-source, offline AI interpretation is the missing layer.

"OreAcle proposes an open-source, local-first interpretation and copilot layer on top of existing mining sensor infrastructure. The sensors and the telemetry are there; the free, offline tool that turns raw numbers into a prioritized, plain-English decision is not."
1
Dashboards show numbers, not decisions

Existing monitoring systems excel at data collection and display. Most don't translate readings into a prioritized action or explain the underlying risk in plain language the operator can act on immediately.

2
Open-source mine AI is sparse

Mining AI tools are mostly proprietary, subscription-based, or require cloud connectivity not reliably available underground. Free, auditable, fully offline alternatives don't exist at this level of integration.

3
Operators need a plain-English copilot

A supervisor should be able to ask "Is Zone B safe right now?" and get a direct, context-aware answer without leaving the console or consulting a manual. That interface doesn't exist as a free, offline tool.

04
The Solution

Sense, Think, Explain, Act — all running locally at $0.

01

Offline-first

Runs entirely on a laptop or ruggedized edge machine. No internet required. No data leaves the mine network. Works at any depth, in any connectivity environment.

02

$0 AI runtime cost

Isolation Forest (scikit-learn) for anomaly detection. Llama 3.2 3B via Ollama for plain-English alerts and copilot chat. Models downloaded once, run forever free.

03

Mine-specific intelligence

Threshold rules for CO, NO₂, vibration, temperature, and airflow. Ventilation-on-Demand fan setpoints driven by real-time gas readings, occupancy, and zone status.

04

Lightweight hardware

Raspberry Pi Pico W at ~$40 all-in as the edge sensor node. The same software pipeline accepts any JSON-emitting sensor — industrial hardware is a drop-in swap.

05
Economics

The AI cost model matters for 24/7 industrial deployments.

Cloud AI APIs
  • Per-token billing — unpredictable cost at 24/7 scale
  • Requires reliable internet connectivity underground
  • Sensor data transmitted off-site (sovereignty risk)
  • Vendor lock-in; pricing changes without notice
  • Latency depends on network quality and API availability
OreAcle — Local AI
  • $0/month — models run free once downloaded
  • Fully offline — no connectivity required underground
  • All sensor data stays on-site, no external exposure
  • Open-source, fully auditable, no vendor dependency
  • Sub-second response on commodity CPU hardware
